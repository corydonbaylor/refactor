English

There was a knock on the door. I hurriedly got up to answer. Jon, Kelsey, Megan and I had been waiting a suspiciously long time for room service to bring the dessert we had ordered. Truth be told, Jon wasn't exactly sure what he had ordered. We were deep in the mountains of central Mexico, in an old hacienda-turned-hotel outside of Queretaro called Hacienda Galindo, and essentially no one there spoke English.

A well dressed waiter entered the room balancing a massive tray containing two large plates topped with silver lids, a somewhat grand conveyance for what we hoped would be a bowl of ice cream and some pastries. We lifted the lid to find an assortment of ice creams, cakes, and to our surprise, two plates of tomato and Alfredo pasta.

When you don't speak the local language you are reduced to something resembling a state of adolescence, unable to fully express what you want to those around you. You become like a toddler, aimlessly drifting from sight to sight, only able to express your desires in the simplest terms, pointing and saying things like "I want the cake, please give me." We were bloated, wide eyed beings who needed help finding the bathroom, who couldn't drink the water without getting sick, and who couldn't tell the taxi where we exactly wanted to be dropped off.

Typically, in a touristy part of the world, there is an implicit pact between the local and tourist. The locals will tolerate your foolishness, gently helping you to relearn how to interact with the world, sometimes even going so far as to pretend that you had miraculously assimilated to local customs in record time and were indistinguishable from the locals themselves—all in exchange for a month's saving spent in a night's time.

We were not in the touristy part of Mexico however. The hotel staff did not speak slow and simple. Bottled water without ice was not the default. No one was there to speak broken English upon hearing us speak broken Spanish, and as Megan and I walked the cobblestone streets of Queretaro, looking for the famed plazas and stone aqueduct that cut through the city center, we did not hear a word of English spoken.
San Miguel

We awoke to the sound of church bells in the main square. We had booked a hotel about a block away from there. Due to a lack of working air-conditioning, we had left the windows open to let the cool mountain air in. And while that kept the room cool, it also let in much of the city sound.

We put on our clothes and walked out into the street in search of food and bottled water. Cars rattled down the cobble stone road in front of our hotel as we headed around the corner to a somewhat famous churro shop.

San Miguel is a city dominated by its churches and squares. It used to be a bustling center of the silver trade—its population swelling to be greater than that of New York City in the 1700s—but following the expulsion of much of the Spanish population in the Mexican revolution, the town declined, freezing much of its housing stock in the 19th century.

As a result, the town remains a spiderweb of steep cobblestone streets, lined with single file sidewalks and short stucco houses, washed in red, orange, and ochre paint. The windows and doors are framed with wrought iron and hewn wood, with the occasional bougainvillea vine falling down its side. The whole thing was built into the side of a mountain, with a gradient of perhaps five hundred feet from one side of town to the other. The Spanish imposed a grid across the terrain, so when you stand at the top, you can clearly see the other end of town rolling down the hill. It rivals anything in Spain in its beauty but more than that it's remarkable for the old town's size and completeness.

It's odd to think that the loss of the silver trade saved the fabric of the city. As it so often does, a steep population decline accompanied the loss of the town's principle industry. No one really lived in San Miguel between 1850 and 1950. It became a bit of a Mexican Pompeii, preserved by economic hardship rather than natural disaster.

That morning we were heading to El Mirrador, an overlook perched on the side of a mountain where we could see the whole city. I recognized that we were walking up the street our shuttle had careened down on the way in from Queretaro. The streets are so narrow there that if you stuck your hand out the window, you could run it along the stucco of the houses. Frankly, I was a bit amazed our shuttle did not sideswipe a passerby as we descended the steep cobblestone streets into the city.

As much as I would like to say that El Mirrador offered the most amazing views of the city, that it took my breath away with its grand vistas, or even that I understood a bit more about the fabric and layout, it did none of those things. San Miguel is an old city, one best experienced at the human level. It is better experienced walking through it than walking away from it.

And so we had more fun walking back, balancing ourselves on the narrow sidewalks, and stopping to take pictures of the principle cathedrals framed by the old townhouses. We wondered what it would be like to retire here. I couldn't imagine walking through the city when it's raining heavily, and water rushes down the mountainside and cobblestone streets, turning the carefully laid grid into a gutter. And what would we do for food? There doesn't appear to be a grocery store in the old city, just a market with improperly stored meats and vegetables. Plus we couldn't drink the water.

No we couldn't live in San Miguel, but it was fun to imagine.

It was getting dark so we walked to Jardin Allende, a cobblestone square at the center of town with manicured trees, a fountain, a gazebo, wrought iron benches, and roving packs of Mariachi. We could hear the sounds of kids laughing, of party goers singing in bars, and the gentle patter of couples chatting. A street dog weaved through the crowd, hoping to get scraps of the food sold by vendors in the park. La Parroquia de San Miguel Arcángel, a massive neo-gothic cathedral painted a light pink color sat illuminated in the dark across from the city square.

Against Megan's forceful protest, I hired a mariachi to sing her a song. They asked what type of song I wanted. I told them something happy. Megan beamed with a mixture of embarrassment and joy as the mariachi sang, and a crowd surrounded us. She leaned against me and we looked up at the cathedral. The cool mountain air drew her close. The song ended, and we walked to the hotel. We could still hear the mariachi sing there.

Tulum

I was drenched in sweat. I was still dealing with the after effects of food poisoning. Every ounce and pore of my body felt overworked and overheated. I swigged a lustful gulp from a large bottled water bought from a street merchant. We had only gotten it a few minutes ago, but it was already warm. I looked at the white stone facade of a pyramid set against the coast, bits of green moss growing between the cracks in the steps.

How was anyone able to build something like this in a place like this?

Megan and I had both gotten food poisoning the night at our all inclusive. I can pinpoint the meal.

What I didn't realize about all inclusives is that staying at one is a bit like staying on a cruise ship. They control everything you do and everything you eat. And if they have bad food or do not offer bottled water on the premises--like ours did not--than you are essentially doomed to eat and drink that rotten food and tainted water.

Our all inclusive allowed us one "restaurant" night during our stay, which we were required to choose at check in. We choose the "Italian" restaurant. The contrast between the pomp of the service and the deplorable state of the food felt like an elaborate and cruel joke. It was as if someone who had only read about eating in books decided to open a restaurant. There was a hostess who checked to see if we had reservations, a waiter to take our order from lamented menus, and the most unappetizing and confusing meal I have ever seen in my life.

I ordered the steak, thinking that it was so simple in its execution that it would be too difficult to mess up. Megan got a pizza, topped with ham. The steak arrived unnaturally soft and chewy. It seemed to melt in my mouth in a suspicious rather than appetizing way. The ham on the pizza floated like chunks of vomit on a sea of florescent cheese.

The waiter came by to ask what we thought.

I felt like saying, "this is legitimately the worse meal I have ever had" But instead told him that it was great but we weren't very hungry.

We left. I wondered if the restaurant staff felt silly putting on bowties and black vests to serve unsuspecting foreigners microwaved pizzas and mystery meat.

Even if we did not get some of the most extensive diarrhea that I--a crohn's disease patient--have ever experienced before, it would still be the worst meal I have ever seen.

As you might suspect, we missed our 7am departure for Chichen Itza, but by noon, our stomachs had quieted and upon Megan's insistence, we took a resort cab to Tulum.

And so we found ourselves in the Yucatan jungle, sore and torn up, with little food and water in our stomachs and sweat dripping from every pore. The humidity hung like a cloud as we inched from pyramid to pyramid. I read a plaque saying that there was no natural fresh water source other than the cenotes, which are a sort of natural well that exposes stagnate groundwater. I read how the mayans pulled huge chunks of granite from far away quarries without the use of the wheel, which they had not yet discovered. How they moved those massive stones, drinking that putrid water, in this rancid weather astounds me.

I looked up at a pyramid built along the edge of a cliff overlooking the salty blue Caribbean below, gulls circling overhead, bugs hissing and clicking in the undergrowth. What a rugged people to cut such a civilization in such hostile terrain.

Mexico City

Between Art Nouveau department stores and office buildings, lost ruins bubble up through the thick muddy sediments of the buried lake, like haunted reminders of vanquished Aztec domains. There the dormant remains of Quetzalcoatl, frozen in stone, guards the crumbled ruins of the great pyramid, the religious center of kingdom built from the blood of conquest and human sacrifice.

But those times are long past, and on top of those Aztec ruins now rises a built environment more akin to a mediterranean kingdom. Measuring over 500,000 square feet and bounded by palaces and a cathedral, Zocalo is the de facto city center, and we met our guide only a few blocks south. A diminutive, thin pale man with a red beard, he guided us around the city core.

To state the obvious, Mexico City has a distinct political history from the United States, and history tends to view its different governments more as an oppressor of its people rather than an extension of their will. That tradition likely traces back to the Aztecs themselves, who famously were defeated by an alliance of disgruntled tribes assembled by Hernan Cortez.

Much of the recent visual identity and charm of Mexico City traces back to Portfolio Diaz, a dictator who rose to power after Mexican independence. He saw the construction of many Art Nouveau masterpieces across the city including the Gran Hotel Ciudad de Mexico. Originally opened as a department store, it features a towering stain glass ceiling, meticulously laid out by French artisan Jacques Gruber in 1908. Our guide pointed out curved, elfish rails that run along the different levels and the elevator caged by a lattice of brushed wrought iron, rising from the polished floor to that magnificent ceiling.

A bit away from the Zocalo, down on Avenida Francisco Madero rests the Casa de los Azulejos, a mansion covered in porcelain tiles reminiscent of the Portugese tradition. Legend has it that the beautiful blue tiles that adorn the house came about due to a Mexican idiom and a familial dispute. Back in the 18th century, an unnamed son of Don Luis spent too much time partying and not enough time working, leading his father to tell him "Jamas llegaras a tender una casa de azulejos", meaning you will never amount to having a house of tiles. That insult served as catalyst for change, eventually resulting in the construction of the house and the creation of the saying.

Later, we snaked back to the Zocalo. The National Cathedral commands the northern end of the square. Visually it dominates the space as the Avenida 20 de Noviembre terminates across from it and its three towers rise the highest in the square. Inside the church sits a spectacular altar of gold and carved wooden saints. But along one the grand pillars that support the church, our guided pointed out a bevy of scientific equipment to measure how unevenly the church is sinking into the ground.

Cortez ordered the church to be built on top of the ruins of Templo Mayor, which itself is built on the muddy, wet land of a filled in lake. And ever since then, the lake has slowly been reclaiming all that is built on top. So down the cathedral sinks into the soil that buried the Aztecs. Once triumphant it will return to the earth, an empire built on sand.

The Neighborhoods

Every great liberal Latin American city needs a walkable, turn of the century neighborhood where the nations elite can live a European lifestyle while cooking up decidedly anti European ideals.

Here the downwardly mobile sons and daughters of the nations elite can tag the walls with faux revolutionary slogans like "Gringo Afuera" and "Aprende Espanol". That is to say, Roma Norte is not so different than any young person hot spot in the United States.

Politics aside, it is quite lovely. Originally built under Porfirio Diaz between the 1890s and 1910s as a refuge for the nation's well to do, the dictator had a flare for the European, and prompted that feel as a symbol of modernization and civilization. Perhaps most impressive are the massive tree lined boulevards, Avenida Alvaro Obregon and Avenida Amsterdam, the second of which is a ring road that encapsulates Parque Mexico. Unlike the parkways in New York where the parkland is on the sides of the streets, there a linear park with lush ferns, trees, and flowering bushes runs through the center of the median.

In fact, the pedestrian space for these streets is a large as that reserved for cars, and we could see a constant drumbeat of joggers weaving through pedestrians. Calle de Orizaba serves as the north-south divider and every few blocks it opens onto a small square, my favorite of which, Plaza Rio de Janeiro, has a beautiful fountain with a massive replica of Michelangelo's David in the center.

If Roma Norte is the young hipster hotspot of CDMX, then Coyoacan is the old money haven from which their parents send the rent. Here the streets are quiet and cobbled. It is more residential, and there are considerably less cafes with outdoor seating spilling in the street.

Our guide walked us through the different squares and streets, pointing out the bright red Bougainvillea vines and lavender purple Jacaranda. This was the land of Diego Rivera and Frida Kahlo, and it is easy to see the laid back, traditional culture that so inspired them here.

We ended our tour at Plaza de la Conchita in front of a faded yellow chapel from the very beginnings of the Spanish Mission in the 1520s. He told us how this neighborhood was one of the few pre-Columbian left in the city, the vast majority of which was still a lake upon the Spanish arrival. Here was the base of Henan Cortez after the fall of Tenochtitlan in 1521, just a little under thirty years after Columbus arrived in the New World.

"Here the story of the Latin America begins," he said. "There are three things that bind us as latinos. First, we are a mix of the native and Spanish, at least about two thirds of us are here in Mexico are. Second, of course, is the Spanish language. And third, Catholicism. All of which collide here in Coyoacan."

Teotihuacan

The old rulers had been overthrown, and strange pale beastly creatures had arrived at the head of a great alliance of Aztec vassal states, determined to overthrow the Aztecs and end the practice of human sacrifice.

But the cosmic debt must be paid. At the beginning of time, the god Nanhuatzin sacrificed himself by leaping into the a great fire, creating but not sustaining the movement of the sun. The motion of the heavens and the stability of this world requires a similar sacrifice from us. Life force or tonalli derived from human blood and hearts. Otherwise the world will go dark.

These pale men tore down the temples, ripped down our statues, beheaded the great serpent, and crushed and buried Huey Tzompantli, a site of great power, where more than 600 hundred skulls— women, children, and men alike — were mortared together with blood and concrete, a physical bulwark against eternal darkness.

Their demands and culture feel so alien and foreign to us yet a common vein runs through. How odd it is that they condemn our sacrifices, but their own God sacrificed his only son Christ so that all of us could live in heaven.

With the capital fallen, thousands of us gather at the ancient ruins of Teotihuacan, so that we may eagerly repent for our failure to stop these deranged men. We wait on the ancient steps for eternal darkness to swallow the earth and for the great cycle to turn again, and bring the universe to slip into something new.

Across the vast span of human civilization, there are few cultures that feel as foreign to our Western sensibilities than the Aztecs. Theirs was an apocalyptic society that was convinced that the world was always on the precipice of ending, held off only by human sacrifice. And from that organizing principle, an entire civilization developed. Special wars, called the flower wars, were fought with neighboring tribes with the express and likely sole purpose to capture sacrificial victims. Great warriors played ōllamaliztli, which was a mix between soccer and basketball with the added twist that the winners would often get sacrificed.

It's easy to pass normative judgements about ancient and foreign civilizations. But of course, it is a great mistake. While it is easy to denounce a morality so different from our own, it is harder to pass judgment on the people who lived within it. To state the obvious, these were people optimizing for a set of moral constraints that arose independently and differently from our own. And who were in day to day interactions were basically good people.

As Megan and I looked across the Avenue of the Dead atop the Pyramid of the Moon, I couldn't help but picture the huddles masses of Aztec refugees who fled here following the collapse of Tenochtitlan. Terror turning to confusion as the sun rose day after day. How it must have felt as the cruel irony set in that human sacrifice was proven to be long forgotten lie.

And here we stood, in the ruins of a great city, once one of the largest in the entire world. At the heart of a civilization who development occurred entirely separate from the rest of humanity, cleaved by vast oceans and desert, growing more complex in isolation, and whose social history was arrested at first contact with interlopers from the Old World. A complete and utter civilizational collapse. How strange and fascinating the human endevaor can be.

Lucha Libre

Like a damsel in distress from some medieval Spanish yarn, Megan waved to me atop a flight of stairs, trapped behind a line of Mexican police. She was trapped there amongst a gaggle of the craning women who dutifully waited for their men to pass security at Arena Mexico.

Our phones were near dead and our guide had told us to get an uber back from the event. Arena Mexico is not in the best part of town. Much like American cities, Mexico City has a little ring of dilapidated warehouses and buildings circling its core. Lucha Libre is a tenant in such a district and, I suppose, a last survivor of vacant store fronts and disinvestment.

"Either these tickets are really expensive or really really cheap," I had told Megan a few hours before after buying some last minute seats on Ticketmaster, unsure if I was paying in pesos or dollars.

They were cheap. Real cheap.

Cheap enough that I had to use a separate entrance than the expensive floor seats that most tourists purchase. Mexico can be an oddly traditional place because for some reason, they separate men and women when going through security, and as the fan base for men in tights pile driving each other into the canvas tends to be more male, men have a much longer security experience to endure.

I say experience rather than line because it was not a line. It was a horde of sweaty, chattering men pushing forward in one big undulating wave toward the entrance doors. Doors, which open directly upon a flight of stairs, atop which Megan was so bashfully waving.

After I had gotten through security, we climbed flight after flight of stairs until we reached the very top. There we opened onto the arena. The seats were steep concrete steps, traunched into three levels, separated by thin metal railings. The crowd absolutely roared as masked men flew about the ring, the sound swelling as it echoed around the concrete box.

This was a place of cheap beers, intense fandom, and very little in the way of building codes. This was Mexico at its most raw and authentic.
